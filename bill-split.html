And My Share Is $_?

我的那份是多少錢？

On the delicate, decades-long, completely unspoken agreement among friends that everyone pays the same — and why, finally, it might be time to say something.

Food· 8 min read · 八分鐘· Entry No. 001

✦ ✦ ✦

So. Here's the thing. You've been going to dinner with the same group of friends for years. Maybe it's every Friday. Maybe it's once a month, a standing reservation, a ritual. You sit down. You order. Some people get the lobster. Some people get the soup. And then the bill comes — and without anyone saying a word, without any discussion, without even a pause — it gets divided. Equally. Down the middle. Every single time.

這種事情你懂的。帳單來了，大家看都不看，直接除以人數。
從來沒有人說什麼。因為這就是「我們的方式」。

And here's what's interesting. Nobody decided this was the arrangement. It just — became the arrangement. The way certain things do, in friendships, in families, in offices. You don't sign a contract. You just do it once, and then you do it again, and then suddenly you've been doing it for six years and it is simply The Way Things Are. The person who ordered the $25 pasta pays the same as the person who ordered the $50 ribeye plus two glasses of wine. Every month. Without fail. Without comment.

Until one day — and this is the part I find genuinely fascinating — someone starts to notice. Maybe it's you. Maybe you've always noticed and just never said anything because what are you going to say? You don't want to be the person who makes dinner weird. Nobody wants to be that person. And so you say nothing. You pay your equal share. You go home. And you think about it the whole drive back.

The bill, divided equally, is not really about money.
It is a small, recurring test of whether anyone in the group
will be the one to say the thing nobody is saying.
帳單平分，從來不只是錢的問題。

Today we're going to talk about that thing. The unspoken arrangement. The equal split. And — more importantly — three ways you might actually go about changing it. Because it can be changed. It just requires a little strategy, a little courage, and depending on which approach you choose, possibly a very good sense of humour.

Solution One · 第一個方法 · With Humour

The "I Just Downloaded an App" Gambit

「我剛下載了一個 App」策略

01

Here's the beauty of living in the age of technology: you can blame the app. You show up to dinner, you wait until the bill arrives, and then — with the energy of someone who has just discovered something genuinely delightful — you pull out your phone and say: "Oh! Has everyone tried Splitwise? I just downloaded it this week, it's actually kind of amazing, it tracks everything, it knows who ordered what, can we just try it once? Just to see?"

Note the enthusiasm. Note the innocence. You are not challenging the arrangement. You are not making a point. You are simply a person who has downloaded an app and would like to share the experience with friends. The app is doing the math. The app is being fair. You are just standing there, delighted by technology, completely blameless.

You can also supplement this approach with a light, self-deprecating remark — something like "I know, I know, I'm that person now" — which immediately defuses any tension and makes everyone laugh. Nobody gets annoyed at a person who is laughing at themselves. This is ancient social wisdom. Use it freely.

重點是：讓 App 當壞人。你只是個科技愛好者，什麼都不知道。

Solution Two · 第二個方法 · With Tact

The Gentle Reframe, Before You Even Sit Down

還沒坐下，就先輕輕說清楚

02

The most tactful move is to handle this before the bill ever arrives. Not with a speech. Not with a declaration. Just a quiet, warm, almost offhand comment as you're all settling in and looking at the menu. Something like: "I was thinking — since we're all ordering different things tonight, should we just each get what we want and pay for our own? That way nobody feels like they have to hold back."

What you're doing here is giving everyone permission. You're not saying the old system was unfair. You're not naming anyone. You are simply presenting a new frame — one where paying for your own food is framed not as an act of accounting, but as an act of freedom. Now everyone can order what they actually want without the silent calculation of whether it's worth it given the split.

The key word is "tonight." You're not announcing a permanent policy change. You're suggesting something for this particular evening. And if it goes well — which it will — it quietly becomes the new normal. No confrontation required. No feelings hurt. Just a gentle pivot, executed with grace.

在菜單還沒點之前說，是最有禮貌的時機。
讓大家覺得這是一種自由，而不是一種指責。

Solution Three · 第三個方法 · Direct & Clear

The Honest Conversation, With Numbers

直接說清楚，附上數字

03

Sometimes the kindest thing you can do is simply be honest. Not aggressive. Not accusatory. Just clear. You wait for a calm moment — maybe before dinner, maybe over a casual coffee — and you say something like: "Hey, I've been thinking about how we split the bill when we go out. I feel a bit uncomfortable paying the same amount when we all order such different things. Would you all be open to each person paying for what they actually ordered? I think it'd just make things feel more fair for everyone."

That's it. No drama. No blame. Just a clear, honest statement of how you feel and a concrete proposal for how to fix it. Friends who care about you will hear this and think — actually, yes, that makes sense. And if anyone pushes back, you have the numbers ready.

Here's a real scenario. Six friends at dinner. Here's what everyone ordered:

Sarah    — Soup + House salad        $22
James    — Ribeye + 2 glasses wine   $58
Mei      — Pasta + sparkling water   $27
Tom      — Lobster + cocktail       $65
Linda    — Chicken + iced tea       $30
You      — Noodle soup + water      $18
Total (before tax & tip):     $220
Equal split per person:     $36.67
Pay for what you ordered:   $18 + your share of tip

Under the equal split, you pay $36.67 for an $18 meal. That's more than double. Over twelve dinners a year, that's roughly $223 extra you're contributing toward other people's ribeye and lobster. Said plainly and warmly, with a receipt in front of everyone, this is hard to argue with.

The direct approach works because it respects everyone at the table — including you. Fairness is not an awkward topic among real friends. And any friend who gets genuinely upset that you'd like to pay for your own noodle soup is a conversation worth having, one way or another.

直接說，是最尊重朋友的方式。
真正的朋友不會因為公平而生氣。

Here's the thing about all three of these approaches. They all work. And they all require the same ingredient — the willingness to be the person who says something. That's the hard part. Not the words. Not the math. Just the moment of deciding that your comfort, and your wallet, and your sense of fairness are worth one slightly awkward sentence at a dinner table.

The equal split has survived this long because everyone assumed everyone else was fine with it. The moment one person speaks up, the whole arrangement is up for renegotiation. And in my experience, once it's said — once it's finally, gently, honestly said — most people around the table quietly exhale and think: thank goodness someone finally brought that up.

說出來，需要一點勇氣。
但通常說出來之後，你會發現——
其實大家都在等有人開口。
那個人，就是你。
