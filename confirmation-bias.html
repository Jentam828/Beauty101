Okay, Fine. I Have Confirmation Bias.

好吧，我承認。我有確認偏誤。

An uncomfortable look in the mirror — at my money, my politics, and the carefully curated echo chamber I've been calling "staying informed."

Personal Therapy· 8 min read · 八分鐘· Entry No. 004

🪞 🧠 😬

I've spent a lot of time in my life being absolutely certain I was right. About money. About politics. About the kind of person who disagrees with me on either. And here's the uncomfortable thing I've had to sit with recently: a significant portion of that certainty was not wisdom. It was architecture. I had built, brick by brick, an information environment perfectly designed to tell me what I already believed — and then I congratulated myself for being so well-informed. That's confirmation bias. And yes, I have it. We all do. The only question is whether you're willing to look at it.

我花了很多時間確信自己是對的。
關於錢。關於政治。關於那些跟我意見不同的人。
而最近我不得不面對一個令人不舒服的事實：
那份確定感，很大一部分不是智慧——而是建築。
我一磚一瓦地建造了一個資訊環境，完美地設計來告訴我我已經相信的事。

Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, and remember information in a way that confirms what you already believe — while unconsciously filtering out evidence that contradicts it. It's not stupidity. It's not even laziness. It's how the human brain manages a world with too much information and not enough time. The brain takes a shortcut. The shortcut feels like conviction. You end up thinking you've done your research when what you've actually done is assembled a very convincing case for your pre-existing conclusion.

確認偏誤是一種傾向：我們傾向於尋找、解讀和記住能夠確認自身信念的資訊，
同時在無意識中過濾掉與之矛盾的證據。
這不是愚蠢，也不是懶惰。這是人腦在應對資訊過載時走的捷徑。
那個捷徑感覺像是信念。你以為自己做了功課，其實只是在為已有的結論蒐集證據。

I'm guilty across several categories — first impressions, investing, health choices, politics — but let's not boil the ocean. Two areas hit close enough to home that I can't look away. Personal finance. And politics. Both of which I have managed, with impressive consistency, to consume almost entirely in the direction of what I already thought.

第一區 · 金融與投資

Area One: Money

— 💰 —

In the early 1990s, I discovered FIRE — Financial Independence, Retire Early. This was before it had a name, before there were subreddits and podcasts and influencers with spreadsheets. There were just a few people on the early internet asking questions that nobody around me was asking: How much do you actually need to retire early? How do you calculate your net worth? How do you make your money outlast you? I was electrified. I had found my people. And then, very quietly, I built an information diet almost entirely out of content that confirmed the answer I had already decided on.

Every article I sought out started from the premise that early retirement was not just possible but obviously correct. Every calculator I ran assumed favourable market returns. Every blog I followed was written by someone who had already done it and was now telling you why you could too. This felt like research. It was not research. It was a support group that happened to use spreadsheets.

  • Articles questioning the 4% safe withdrawal rate under low-return environments 質疑低報酬環境下4%提領率是否安全的文章
  • Research on sequence-of-returns risk in the early retirement years — the years when a bad market does the most damage 關於提早退休初期「報酬順序風險」的研究——市場在這幾年下跌傷害最大
  • Healthcare cost projections for early retirees in the US before Medicare eligibility at 65 美國65歲Medicare資格之前，提早退休者的醫療費用預測
  • Stories from people who tried early retirement and returned to work — not failures, just a fuller picture 那些嘗試提早退休後重返職場的人的故事——不是失敗，只是更完整的圖像
  • The specific costs of retiring early in an expensive region — Bay Area numbers, not Montana numbers 在高消費地區提早退休的實際費用——灣區的數字，不是蒙大拿的數字
The consequence isn't that early retirement is wrong — it isn't. The consequence is that I arrived at major financial decisions with genuine conviction built on selectively gathered evidence. Confidence is useful. Confidence assembled from a one-sided reading list is something else. It's a guess wearing a suit.

後果不是提早退休是錯的——它不是。
後果是我帶著真實的信念做出了重大財務決定，而那份信念是建立在選擇性蒐集的證據上。
信心是有用的。從片面閱讀清單中組裝出來的信心，是另一回事。那是穿著西裝的猜測。
第二區 · 政治與新聞

Area Two: Politics

— 📰 —

Let me be precise about this, because vagueness is how we avoid the uncomfortable part. The news I consume leans in a particular direction. The articles I read, the podcasts I listen to, the commentators I follow — they are, with very few exceptions, people who share my general political beliefs, my party, and the policies I care about. I have curated an information environment so well-matched to my priors that being "well-informed" and "having my existing beliefs validated" have become functionally the same activity.

And the thing is — I know this. I've known it for years. I tell myself I read widely. I tell myself I consider other perspectives. But if I'm being precise: I read widely within a fairly narrow band. I consider perspectives that are maybe ten degrees different from mine, not perspectives that are genuinely opposed. The brain is a very good lawyer. It will find a reason to dismiss any evidence that threatens the verdict it has already reached.

The problem with a well-curated information diet
is that it feels like wisdom.
You're not uninformed. You're selectively informed,
which is a much harder problem to fix
because you don't feel like you have a problem.
精心策劃的資訊飲食的問題在於，它感覺像智慧。
你不是無知，而是選擇性地知情——這更難解決，因為你感覺不到自己有問題。
  • Policy critiques from serious people who don't share my politics but have legitimate points 來自不認同我政治立場、但觀點有理有據的認真人士的政策批評
  • Economic arguments from the other side that don't fit the story I've already accepted 來自另一方的經濟論點，與我已接受的敘事不符
  • Evidence that policies I support have had unintended negative consequences 我支持的政策產生了意外負面後果的證據
  • The possibility that the people I disagree with politically are not all acting in bad faith 我在政治上不認同的人，並非全都出於惡意行事的可能性
The consequence of political confirmation bias isn't just personal. When millions of people are doing this simultaneously — all of us consuming only what confirms our own side — the result is a society where nobody is actually talking to each other, everyone believes they are the reasonable one, and compromise becomes not just difficult but conceptually impossible. I am not the cause of political polarization. But I am a small, willing participant in it, every single day. That's the part I don't love admitting.

政治確認偏誤的後果不只是個人的。
當數百萬人同時這樣做——我們都只消費確認自己立場的內容——
結果是一個沒有人真正在互相交談的社會，每個人都認為自己是理性的那方，
而妥協不只是困難，而是在概念上不可能。
我不是政治極化的原因。但我每天都是其中一個小小的、自願的參與者。

I don't have a tidy answer here. I'm not going to tell you I've fixed it, because I haven't. I'm not going to give you a five-step plan for eliminating confirmation bias, because anyone selling you that is probably confirming your bias that self-improvement is simple. What I have is this: I know it's happening. I can name the mechanism. I can catch myself mid-scroll thinking — wait, am I reading this because it's true, or because it agrees with me?

That pause. That small, uncomfortable moment of self-awareness — that's the whole first step. Not the solution. Just the beginning of not being completely asleep at the wheel. It doesn't sound like much. But given how many people never get there at all, I'll take it.

The goal isn't to have no beliefs. The goal is to hold them a little more loosely. To occasionally read the thing that makes you uncomfortable. To sit with the possibility — just the possibility — that you might be partially, annoyingly, inconveniently wrong. About the money. About the politics. About the story you've been telling yourself for thirty years.

我沒有整齊的答案。我沒有辦法告訴你我已經解決了這個問題，因為我沒有。
我有的是：我知道它正在發生。我能說出這個機制的名字。
我能在滑手機的中途停下來想——等等，我讀這個是因為它是真的，
還是因為它同意我的看法？

那個停頓。那個小小的、不舒服的自我覺察時刻——那就是第一步的全部。
不是解決方案。只是開始不再對方向盤完全睡著。
目標不是沒有信念。而是輕輕地握著它們。
偶爾讀一些讓你不舒服的東西。
坐在那個可能性裡——你可能是錯的。關於錢。關於政治。
關於你對自己講了三十年的故事。
