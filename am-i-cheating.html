Am I Cheating?

這算作弊嗎？

On discovering AI, the guilt of using it, the thrill of what it produces, and the deeply uncomfortable question of whether any of this is still mine.

Personal Therapy· 8 min read · 八分鐘· Entry No. 002

✦ ✦ ✦

It was November 2022. My father had just passed away. I was sitting at my desk with a blank document in front of me and a eulogy due in four days and absolutely nothing coming out. I had thoughts — plenty of them, too many of them, all tangled up in the specific mess that grief makes of language. A friend mentioned this new tool. ChatGPT. I typed in my memories of my father — the ones I could articulate and the ones I was still trying to — and asked it to help me find the shape of what I wanted to say. Something came back. I stared at it for a long time. Then I kept going. I delivered the eulogy. I have not fully untangled my feelings about it since.

二零二三年十一月。朋友傳來一個連結。
我輸入了一些東西。螢幕回應了我。
我盯著它看了很久，像在看魔術表演。
然後我忘記睡覺了。

I want to be clear about who I was before that night. I was a person who had strong opinions about technology — specifically, that it was fine for other people and personally not for me, thank you. I did not use Siri because Siri always misheard me and I found the experience humiliating for both of us. I had strong feelings about autocorrect. Mostly negative. And here I was at midnight, asking a computer to explain the French Revolution, just to see what it would say, which was frankly better than what I remembered from school.

I should say something honest here. My father passed away in November 2022 — the same month ChatGPT launched, which I mention not because the timing is ironic but because it is simply true. Consumed by grief and completely unable to write, I typed my thoughts and memories about him into this new thing and asked it to help me with his eulogy. The output was good. Genuinely good. And I stood at his funeral and delivered words that were mine in origin but not entirely mine in execution, and I have not fully resolved how I feel about that. I probably never will. Some things you don't get to resolve. You just carry them.

The Thing About New Shiny Things

AI tools arrived the way important things tend to arrive — not gradually, then suddenly, but more like: imperceptibly, imperceptibly, imperceptibly, and then you blink and it's everywhere and everyone is either panicking or pretending they were early adopters all along. Nobody was an early adopter. We were all just suddenly in the middle of it.

Nov 2022
ChatGPT launches — and I use it immediately. Not for fun. My father passed away this month. Consumed by grief, I typed my memories of him into this new tool and asked it to help me write his eulogy. That was my first encounter. Not the most casual introduction. ChatGPT 上線。同一個月，我父親離開了。我用它幫我寫了他的悼詞。這是我第一次使用 AI。不是最輕鬆的開場。
Early 2024
Claude arrives. Gemini arrives. The tools multiply. The excuses to not try them run out. Claude 出現。Gemini 出現。工具越來越多，理由越來越少。
2024–2025
AI is now in everything. Your search engine, your email, your spreadsheets, your car, your doctor's waiting room, and apparently your blog, if you are me. AI 進入了所有東西。包括這個部落格，如果你是我的話。

The speed of it is the part that nobody quite prepared for. It is not that the tools arrived — tools always arrive. It's that within eighteen months, not using them started to feel like a personal statement. Like refusing to use a dishwasher on principle. Possible, technically. But you will spend a lot of time at the sink, and you will not produce cleaner dishes.

How I Ended Up Here, Writing This, This Way

The blog was my idea. Every word of the brief was mine — the title, the sections, the feeling I wanted it to have, the posts I wanted to write, the restaurants I wanted to recommend, the restaurants I changed my mind about. The opinions are mine. The experiences are mine. The embarrassing parking complaint anecdote that I just shared with strangers on the internet is entirely, unfortunately, mine.

But the sentences — the actual, finished, presentable sentences — arrived with assistance. I described what I wanted. Something wrote it out. I read it, changed things, occasionally deleted entire sections because they sounded too earnest or too polished or too much like a brochure, and asked for something different. It is collaborative in the way that working with a very good editor is collaborative. Except the editor never sighs. The editor never says "are you sure about this?" in a tone that means "I am not sure about this." The editor simply produces another draft and waits.

The ideas are mine. The anxiety about the ideas is mine.
The output exceeded what I would have produced alone.
I am not sure what to do with that information.
想法是我的。焦慮是我的。成果超過了我自己能做到的。我不知道該怎麼處理這件事。

The Part Where I Ask Myself Uncomfortable Questions

1
If I didn't write it word by word, is it still my writing? 如果我沒有一字一句地寫，這還是我的文章嗎？ Probably. A director doesn't operate the camera. An architect doesn't pour the concrete. The vision, the choices, the judgment of what stays and what goes — that's the work. The typing was always the least interesting part.
2
Am I cheating? 這算作弊嗎？ Cheating implies there are rules and a winner. This is a personal blog. The only competition was with my own inertia, which I have clearly beaten. If this is cheating, I would like more opportunities to cheat. I would like a trophy, actually.
3
Am I creative? 我有創意嗎？ I knew I wanted a bilingual Wes Anderson blog about food, travel, and the contents of my head. I knew I wanted it to sound like me, roughly, on a good day. I knew which restaurant to change and why. Creativity is curation as much as it is production. I am curating aggressively.
4
Is this personal, or just factory output? 這是個人的，還是只是工廠流水線的產物？ The auto-generated "checking in on your application" LinkedIn message is factory output. The birthday email your bank sends you is factory output. This is different. Nobody else has my particular combination of opinions about bill-splitting etiquette, Julia Child's CIA years, and the guilt of early retirement. The factory doesn't have a biography. I do.

What I Have Decided, Provisionally

I have decided that the discomfort is the point. If using a new tool felt completely natural and uncomplicated, it would mean I wasn't paying attention. The questions — about authorship and creativity and what "mine" means — are good questions. They deserve the discomfort. They will probably never be fully resolved, which is fine. Most interesting questions aren't.

What I know is this: the blog exists. Before November 2023 it did not exist, and it also would not have existed if I had waited for myself to do it the traditional way. I have been meaning to start a blog since approximately 2011. The math is not in my favour.

部落格存在了。
在我發現 AI 之前，它不存在。
如果我等著用傳統方式寫，它現在也不會存在。
我從 2011 年就想開始寫部落格了。數學不支持我。

The tool doesn't have opinions about Frances restaurant on 17th Street. It doesn't know what it felt like to sit at a table with the same friends for twenty years, dividing a bill that was never quite fair. It doesn't know what it means to leave a job that paid well and then spend a Tuesday morning wondering what, exactly, you're for. It knows what I tell it. I tell it a lot. That's the collaboration.

AI 不知道在十七街那家餐廳是什麼感覺。
它不知道和同一群朋友吃了二十年的飯是什麼感覺。
它只知道我告訴它的事情。
我告訴了它很多。這就是合作。

David Sedaris — whose voice I have borrowed for this post, with apologies and admiration in roughly equal measure — once said that when people ask if his stories are true, he tells them they're true enough. I think about that a lot. This blog is mine enough. My father's eulogy was mine enough. I have decided to stop expecting a cleaner answer than that.

Every generation gets a new tool and spends the first few years feeling guilty about how much they like it. The printing press. The typewriter. Spell-check. Google. Now this. The guilt is not a sign that something is wrong. It is a sign that something has changed, and that you are paying enough attention to notice.

Use the tool. Ask the questions. Keep the biography. That last part is the only part nobody else can do for you.

每個世代都有新工具，然後花幾年時間對自己喜歡它這件事感到愧疚。
罪惡感不是出了什麼問題的徵兆。
它只是說明有些事情改變了，而你夠清醒，注意到了這件事。
用這個工具。繼續問問題。保留你自己的故事。
那最後一部分，沒有人能替你做。
