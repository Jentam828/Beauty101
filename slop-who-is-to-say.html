Slop: Who Is to Say

網路垃圾：誰說了算？

On AI-generated content, what makes writing real, and whether this very post answers the question or simply becomes it.

Personal Therapy· 8 min read · 八分鐘· Entry No. 004

📅 Published · March 28, 2026
🤖 ✍️ 😔
第一節 · 定義

What Is Slop, Exactly

— ✦ —

The internet has a new category of pollution, and it has a name now: slop. Low-quality content generated by AI at scale, published with little or no human judgment applied at any stage of the process. The word is borrowed from farming — what you feed pigs, the stuff left over that nobody wanted — and the analogy is unkind but accurate. Slop is what the information environment looks like when the cost of generating content drops to nearly zero and the incentive to care about its quality drops with it.

網路有了一種新的污染，現在有了名字：垃圾內容（Slop）。
由AI大規模生成、在發布過程中幾乎沒有人工判斷介入的低品質內容。
這個詞借自農業——餵豬的剩料，沒人要的東西——
這個比喻很殘忍，但很準確。

It shows up in Facebook feeds as AI-generated images of soldiers reuniting with dogs, designed to harvest likes from people who don't notice they're looking at something that never happened. It shows up in Amazon's Kindle store as thousands of thin books published under invented names. It shows up as entire websites — with plausible mastheads and confident bylines — that exist only to rank on Google and collect advertising revenue. It shows up as LinkedIn posts that begin "I was nervous walking into that meeting" and are now so recognisably synthetic that people screenshot them as jokes.

The technical definition is straightforward. The moral and aesthetic definition is where things get complicated — and personal. Because here is the thing I have been sitting with, and cannot fully resolve: I think I might be doing it too.

第二節 · 自我審視

Am I Contributing to the Slop?

— ✦ —

This blog exists because I wanted to write. To travel and notice things and put words to them. To think out loud about food and money and the shape of a life in the middle of living it. Those are real impulses. They belong to me. Nobody installed them.

And yet: I use AI to write nearly every post. I bring the experiences, the opinions, the specific detail — the two chairs at Goodwill for under four dollars, the grilled eel in Nakameguro, the retirement math that keeps me up some nights. I bring the direction. But I do not write most of the sentences. An AI does. A good one, attentive to my voice and my preferences, capable of producing something that reads — I think, I hope — like me. But still. Not me typing. Not me choosing each word in the particular way that choosing each word has always meant something.

It is almost too easy. That is the thing I keep returning to. The posts are good — I believe that, I would not publish them if I didn't — but the effort required to produce them is so much less than the effort that writing used to require that I genuinely don't know if the category is the same anymore. Is what I am doing writing? Is it curating? Is it something new that doesn't have a name yet, or something old that now has a machine in the middle of it?

The experiences are mine. The opinions are mine.
The memories are mine. The sentences, mostly, are not.
I have been trying to figure out what that means
for longer than this post has existed.
經歷是我的。觀點是我的。記憶是我的。句子，大多數，不是。
我一直在試著弄清楚這意味著什麼——比這篇文章存在的時間還要長。

What I notice is this: the posts that feel most mine are the ones where I pushed back on the draft. Where I said — that sentence is wrong, that observation is too tidy, that ending is not how I actually feel. The resistance is where my voice lives. The easy version, the version I accept without friction, feels thinner somehow — correct, but not quite true.

So maybe the question is not whether AI wrote the sentences. Maybe the question is whether I was present enough to make them honest. Whether the post is worth sending into the world depends less on who typed it and more on whether anyone actually stood behind it — thought about whether it was true, whether it earned its place, whether it was something or just something that looked like something.

If I didn't write each sentence, does it mean it isn't personal? 如果我沒有寫下每一個句子，它還算是個人的嗎？
If it reads like me but wasn't written by me, whose voice is it? 如果它讀起來像我，但不是我寫的，這是誰的聲音？
Is something worth less because it was easy to make? 一件事因為容易做到，就變得不那麼有價值嗎？
Is it worth sending into the world — or is it just more noise wearing the costume of something personal? 它值得送入世界嗎——還是只是穿著個人外衣的雜訊？
第三節 · 沒有答案

I Don't Have the Answer

— ✦ —

I want to be careful here about the kind of false resolution that essays reach for because readers expect them to land somewhere. The honest thing is that I don't know. I have read enough about AI and authorship and authenticity to know that smart people disagree about all of it, and that the disagreement is not going to be settled by one person writing a blog post — even a person who genuinely wrestled with the question before publishing.

What I do know is that the question matters. Slop is a problem not because AI generated it but because nobody cared enough to stand behind it. The Facebook image designed to harvest engagement has no author, no accountability, no person who looked at it and thought: is this true, is this good, does this deserve to exist? That absence of care is what makes it slop. Not the technology. The indifference.

And I am not indifferent to these posts. I care about them in a way that is hard to fully articulate but easy to feel — the mild anxiety before publishing, the satisfaction when something lands right, the dissatisfaction when it doesn't, the ongoing question of whether the whole project is honest or just clever. That might be enough. Or it might not be. This is where I am, and I do not have a more resolved place to offer.

This post was written in the voice of Ezra Klein — a journalist and thinker I admire for his willingness to sit inside a hard question rather than hurry out of it. Does that mean it isn't my voice? I chose the voice because it matched the weight of what I was trying to say. Whether that makes it more mine or less mine is, perhaps, exactly the question this entire post is asking.

For the record — for anyone reading this a few years from now, when the questions raised here may have clearer answers or may have gotten considerably more complicated — I want it noted that I did not arrive at this easily. I have struggled with it. I struggle with it still. The eulogy I wrote for my father with the help of a machine, in the fog of grief, is still the thing I come back to when I try to understand what AI does and does not take from us. I have not resolved that either. Some things resist resolution. This appears to be one of them.

I kept writing anyway. That is the most honest thing I can say.

留此為據——給幾年後讀到這篇文章的人，
那時這些問題也許有了更清晰的答案，或者變得更加複雜——
我想讓人知道，我沒有輕易到達這裡。
我為此掙扎過。現在仍然在掙扎。
我在悲痛的迷霧中，借助一台機器寫下的父親悼詞，
仍然是我每次試圖理解AI究竟奪走了我們什麼時，
會回想起的那件事。那件事，我也沒有解決。
有些事拒絕被解決。這件事似乎就是其中之一。

儘管如此，我繼續寫了下去。這是我能說的最誠實的話。
